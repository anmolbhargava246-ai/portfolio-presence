When ambiguity costs more than interest rates

Why a technically seamless digital loan product still wasn't getting taken, and what changed when the team shifted from speed-first messaging to clarity-first trust-building.

Activation & onboardingTrust & adoptionBehavioural friction
TL;DR
Sector
FinTech, personal loans, India
Method
In-depth interviews, usability testing, JTBD, journey mapping, cognitive load analysis
Sample
Loan users, drop-offs, and credit users who avoided loans across Tier 1–3 cities
Partnered with
Product, design, marketing, risk
Strategic shift
Speed-first messaging → clarity-first trust-building
The problem

The product was, technically, seamless. The KYC worked. The disbursal worked. The approval times were genuinely fast. And yet adoption sat well below where the team had expected it to be.

The instinct inside the room was that the product needed to be faster. The behavioural read suggested something else.

Speed wasn't the friction point. Users were stalling earlier, on uncertainty about what they were actually signing up for.

Underneath what looked like a usability problem sat an ambiguity problem. The funnel worked. The decision didn't feel safe enough to complete.

The reframe

We reframed the loan journey as three behavioural questions users were asking themselves at each step, replacing the team's flow-based view of the funnel.

Layer 01, Eligibility trust

Will I be judged or rejected before I even understand the terms?

Layer 02, Cost trust

Do I actually know what I'll pay back, and when?

Layer 03, Commitment trust

Can I back out if something changes, without consequence?

What we found
Ambiguity drove more hesitation than the interest rate did

What users were rejecting wasn't the loan itself. It was the ambiguity around what they were committing to. Hidden charges, unclear repayment terms, and vague language triggered more hesitation than the interest rate ever did.

Speed only mattered after trust was established

Fast approval was attractive, though never enough to override the need for clarity. Speed read as pressure when trust hadn't been earned yet.

High cognitive load amplified anxiety

Complex forms and dense information during KYC made users second-guess their decision mid-flow, even when they had originally intended to complete it.

Borrowing carried emotional weight beyond the transactional surface

First-time borrowers relied heavily on family and peer validation before committing. The journey was a social decision much more than an individual one.

What changed
  • Repayment language and fee terminology rewritten across key screens
  • Total cost surfaced earlier so users could commit knowingly
  • Reassurance cues and lender context added during high-anxiety steps
  • Stakeholder narrative shifted from speed-first to clarity-first
Takeaway

Trust runs deeper than UX. It sits across product, brand, and organisation at once, and behavioural trust issues require cross-functional solutions.
